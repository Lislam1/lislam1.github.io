My Teaching Philosophy

To Explore a Beautiful World with Curiosity

Liana Islam

 

I am a biophysicist in training and my subject matter is interdisciplinary. In my teaching career, I have taught a breadth of subjects- mechanics and waves, general physics laboratory, scientific computing, introductory chemistry laboratory, organic chemistry laboratory and general chemistry laboratory courses. I hope to make a career of teaching scientific computing where physics and chemistry students learn how to solve scientific problems using programming languages like C++, python, UNIX, MATLAB and Mathematica. In the process of teaching students to code, I focus on developing the students’ ability to visualize a problem clearly so they can write code to solve it. In the process, they must learn to think critically and get hands-on experience in programming.

To me, teaching is to get students to stop thinking about what must be learned and to think about what they can learn instead. It is about getting them to be curious and open minded. My goal is to get students to approach content with an open mind, free from pre-conceived notions so they can spot gems of insight that are embedded in the terrain of exams and grades. It is the sudden scientific realization that brings joy to my life. For example, when I finally arrive on a model that fits my stopped-flow data I feel a deep sense of accomplishment. I wish for my students to experience this feeling. When I teach students to run experiments or simulations, I make it clear at the beginning of class that it is not important what I think they should be getting as results. It is more important what they think of the results after they make sure they have followed logical steps all through. They must be able to reflect with logic. I find that encouraging openness, critical thinking and student agency makes sure that my students never feel the need to lie to me about results in the lab. We go over their findings together and I help them think about what went right and what went wrong in their data collection methods, calculations and thinking process. I make sure my students focus on correct technique and mathematical calculations and then become accepting of the results. Any assumptions must be grounded in experimental data and not on wishful thinking. I want my students emerge as free-thinking individuals who are dedicated to truth and encourage them to challenge me and even point out when I am wrong. Thus, the whole teaching process becomes an opportunity for my personal growth as well as theirs. I learn with my students.

 

I try to be hands-on, especially in the earlier days of the semester. Because most of the courses I have taught are laboratory courses, I have to make sure the students are learning to carry out tasks accurately and responsibly so that they can safely collect data. I find that students open up more about their shortcomings when the teacher is not trying to avoid them in the name of fostering independence. To me, good training forms the foundation of independence. I prefer teaching on the board- mostly because what I teach does not involve complicated diagrams. If there are complicated diagrams, I may opt for PowerPoint. For teaching mathematics or physics, I feel that the board allows me to deliver content in a slow, digestible manner to students. I think teaching videos are a great tool for independent study and can make course content more accessible but feel that they should be a supplement to in-person instruction- not the principal mode. Much of scientific development takes place trough interpersonal exchange which is key to the educational process.

 

I have taught students in Bangladesh and in the United States. They have come from various backgrounds and varying degrees of preparation. When I deal with a student it is very important for me to know where they are at their learning journey so I can build on what they know. I try to understand what they do not know and what they need to know by asking questions related to the content. The level of detail I go into when explaining content one-on-one depends on their answers. This is one way I try to create an inclusive environment. I try to make sure the student knows that they can approach me without judgment and ridicule- that they are safe to express their thoughts as they think.

 

I realize that not all of my students will end up being scientists. I still train them like they will become scientists eventually. Everyone is entitled to enjoying the secrets of the universe. Getting good grades while doing so helps. I give lots of written feedback on their assignments but am not a harsh grader. Also, I like to keep the element of play in work. It helps that the course I currently teach as a teaching assistant was designed as a laboratory course undertaken as groups. Like playing in a team, working in a group on science projects have benefits that go beyond the lab- like fostering cooperation and teamwork among students. It is wonderful to see how my students learn to become more responsible and communicative humans in the process of solving problems together. When I get the opportunity to teach scientific computing with more autonomy in designing course content, I plan on introducing teamwork in scientific computing as well and teach students how to collaborate in the real world and on GitHub.

 

I want my students to become scientifically thinking, responsible and creative members of society. Hopefully some of them will join research in the field of biophysics. But above all, I want them to be happy well-rounded individuals- confident in their skills and eager to learn more.
